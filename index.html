A Guide to Sedation in the UK Emergency Department

An Infographic Summary of Best Practice for Emergency Physicians

Patient Selection & Assessment

Patient Selection

Indications:

Any diagnostic or therapeutic procedure causing significant pain or anxiety where stillness is required.

Absolute Contraindications:

  • Lack of trained staff or resuscitation facilities.
  • Known severe allergy to agents.

Relative Contraindications (Senior Review):

  • High ASA Status (ASA III+).
  • Predicted difficult airway.
  • Active respiratory compromise (e.g., wheeze).
  • Severe comorbidities (e.g., pulmonary hypertension).

Pre-Sedation Assessment

A systematic, documented assessment is the cornerstone of safe practice.

  • History: Previous anaesthetic issues, comorbidities (esp. OSA), medications, allergies, alcohol/substance use.
  • ASA Status: Most ED sedation is for ASA I (Healthy) or ASA II (Mild systemic disease). ASA III+ requires senior involvement.
  • Airway Exam: The critical question: "Can I bag-mask ventilate this patient?"
    • External features (obesity, beard, trauma).
    • 3-3-2 Rule: Mouth opening (>3), Hyoid-mental (>3), Hyoid-thyroid (>2).
    • Mallampati Score: Class III/IV suggests difficulty.
    • Neck mobility.

RCEM Fasting Guidance

Do not delay procedural sedation in adults or children in the ED based on fasting time. Evidence has not shown that fasting reduces emesis or aspiration risk in ED procedural sedation (especially minimal/moderate).

Grading Sedation Depth

Sedation is a continuum. The goal for most ED procedures is Moderate Sedation to maintain a safety buffer.

  • 1. Minimal Sedation (Anxiolysis): Responds normally to verbal commands. Airway/ventilation unaffected.
  • 2. Moderate Sedation ('Conscious'): Purposeful response to verbal/tactile stimuli. Airway intact, ventilation adequate.
  • 3. Deep Sedation: Responds purposefully to repeated/painful stimuli. Airway may be impaired, ventilation may be inadequate.
  • 4. General Anaesthesia: Unarousable. Airway intervention often required, ventilation frequently inadequate.

The "Rescue" Imperative

You MUST be able to "rescue" a patient from one level deeper than intended.

This means any clinician giving moderate sedation must be competent in managing deep sedation, including advanced airway manoeuvres and effective bag-valve-mask ventilation.

'Ketofol' (1:1 combination)

Balances side effects: Ketamine supports BP, Propofol reduces emergence. Dose 0.5-0.75 mg/kg of each.

Reversal Agents

Caution: Re-sedation risk as reversal agent half-life may be shorter than drug. Requires min. 2 hours post-reversal monitoring.

Performing Safe Sedation: The Workflow

  1. Preparation (SAFE Approach)
    • Staffing: Minimum 3: Sedationist, Proceduralist, dedicated Monitor/Scribe.
    • Area: Resuscitation bay with 360° patient access.
    • Facilities: Oxygen, functional suction, airway trolley, monitor, defib.
    • Equipment: Emergency drugs (inc. reversal agents) ready.
  2. Team Brief & Checklist
    • Formal "Time Out" using a checklist is mandatory.
    • Confirm patient, procedure, consent, roles, and airway plan.
    • Discuss "What if?" rescue strategy (e.g., apnoea plan).
  3. Essential Monitoring
    • Pulse Oximetry, NIBP (q3-5m), 3-lead ECG.
    • Waveform Capnography (ETCO₂): Now standard of care. Provides the earliest warning of hypoventilation/apnoea, long before SpO₂ drops.
  4. Mock Sedation (Ankle Reduction)
    • ASA I patient, reassuring airway. Consent obtained.
    • Team brief in resus bay. Fentanyl + Propofol plan. Apnoea plan verbalised.
    • Full monitoring applied, including capnography. Pre-oxygenate.
    • Give Fentanyl 50mcg. Wait 3 mins.
    • Titrate Propofol 20mg boluses, observing ETCO₂ and patient response.
    • Once at moderate sedation (drowsy, slurring, but responds), proceduralist begins.
    • Sedationist focuses 100% on patient and monitors.
    • Procedure ends, monitoring continues until baseline LOC.

Aftercare & Discharge

Recovery Phase

Monitor in resus-capable area for min. 30 mins post-last dose. Beware of deepening sedation as painful stimulus is removed.

Criteria-Led Discharge

Patient must meet all criteria:

  • Returned to baseline Level of Consciousness.
  • Stable vital signs.
  • SpO₂ >94% on room air.
  • Pain/nausea controlled.
  • Tolerating oral fluids.
  • Mobilising safely to baseline.

Mandatory 24-Hour Rules

Patient MUST be discharged with a responsible adult. They MUST receive written advice to NOT do the following for 24 hours:

  • Drive or operate machinery.
  • Consume alcohol.
  • Make important/legal decisions.
  • Care for dependants alone.

Capacity & Consent

For elective PSA, valid informed consent is mandatory.

Mental Capacity Act (2005)

Applies to patients 16+. Key principles:

  1. Presume capacity unless proven otherwise.
  2. Must support patient to make a decision.
  3. Patient has the right to make an unwise decision.
  4. Any action taken must be in their Best Interests.
  5. Must use the Least Restrictive Option.

Rapid Tranquilisation (RT)

Legal justification (if patient lacks capacity) is based on the MCA and common law (doctrine of necessity) to prevent harm. Treatment must be in their best interests and the least restrictive option (i.e., de-escalation must have failed).

Human Factors

Safety is systemic. Non-technical skills are as vital as technical ones.

Crew Resource Management (CRM)

  • Clear Communication: Use SBAR.
  • Leadership & Followership: Create a flat hierarchy where any team member (inc. nurse) feels empowered to speak up.
  • Situational Awareness: Avoid fixation error (e.g., focusing on the procedure and ignoring the monitor).
  • Workload Distribution: Dedicated roles prevent cognitive overload.

Checklists and Simulation are key tools to improve team dynamics and manage crises.

Rapid Tranquilisation

A last-resort intervention for acute behavioural disturbance with immediate risk to self or others.

  • Exclude Reversible Causes: Hypoxia, hypoglycaemia, head injury, sepsis.
  • De-escalation First: Must be attempted and failed (unless risk is immediate).
  • Monitoring (NICE QS154): Min. every 15 mins for 1st hour, then hourly. Includes vitals, LOC (AVPU), and side effects (e.g., EPSEs).

Complication Management Algorithms

Complication Recognition Immediate Management Steps
Respiratory Depression / Apnoea Reduced RR, absent/flat ETCO₂ waveform. Late sign: ↓SpO₂.
  1. STOP sedative.
  2. Stimulate patient (verbal/tactile).
  3. Perform basic airway manoeuvres (jaw thrust).
  4. Assist ventilation with BVM + 100% O₂.
  5. Consider reversal agent (Naloxone/Flumazenil).
Laryngospasm Inspiratory stridor ("crowing"), paradoxical chest movement, no air entry.
  1. STOP procedure, Call for Help (Anaesthetics).
  2. Apply 100% O₂ with CPAP via tight-fitting mask.
  3. Apply forceful jaw thrust & pressure at Larson's Point.
  4. Deepen sedation with Propofol (0.25-0.5 mg/kg IV).
  5. If hypoxic, give Suxamethonium (1-2 mg/kg IV) & prepare to intubate.
Hypotension Significant drop in BP from baseline (common with Propofol).
  1. STOP sedative (esp. Propofol).
  2. Administer rapid IV fluid bolus (250-500 ml).
  3. Consider passive leg raise.
  4. If unresponsive, give vasopressor (e.g., Metaraminol 0.5mg IV).
Ketamine Emergence Reaction Agitation, confusion, hallucinations, vivid dreams during recovery.
  1. Move to a quiet, low-stimulus environment.
  2. Provide calm, verbal reassurance.
  3. For severe agitation, give Midazolam 1-2 mg IV.
